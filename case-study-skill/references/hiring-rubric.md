# How Design Leaders Read Portfolios

This is calibration context, not the primary lens. A case study should present how someone works. When it does that well, it naturally addresses what reviewers look for.

## What experienced reviewers pay attention to

Whether someone is reviewing portfolios for hiring, mentorship, conference talks, or collaboration, they tend to evaluate along the same five dimensions:

### 1. Problem-solving and judgment
- Did they understand the problem deeply or just execute a brief?
- Did they show constraints and how those shaped decisions?
- Did they explore multiple directions before converging?
- Can they articulate why they killed rejected approaches?

### 2. Craft and execution
- For visual designers: is the work beautiful, systematic, and intentional?
- For UX: are the flows clear, the hierarchy sound, the interactions considered?
- For design engineers: does the shipped product match the design intent? Are details polished?
- For all: does the quality of the case study itself demonstrate care?

### 3. Impact and outcomes
- Are outcomes specific and believable? ("67% to 41%" not "improved 20%")
- Is qualitative impact grounded in evidence? (User quotes, support ticket trends, adoption data)
- Do they acknowledge confounders honestly?
- For unshipped work: do they use proxy outcomes? (Usability tests, stakeholder decisions influenced)

### 4. Strategic and systems thinking
- Did they consider the broader product ecosystem?
- Did they negotiate scope, push back, or make tradeoff decisions?
- Did they think about scalability, edge cases, accessibility?
- Do they connect their work to outcomes beyond their immediate scope?

### 5. Point of view
- Does this person have opinions about design?
- Can you tell what they believe and how that shapes their work?
- Does the case study feel authored or assembled?
- Is there a voice here, or could any designer have written this?

## Patterns that weaken a portfolio

Help the user avoid these. They're not "red flags to game around" but genuine weaknesses in how the work is presented:

1. **No thinking shown.** Only final mockups with no evidence of decisions.
2. **No outcomes or reflection.** Work presented as self-evidently good.
3. **Vague individual contribution.** "We did..." without specifying their role.
4. **Every case study reads the same.** Same structure, same section headers, same tone. This is the "case study factory" pattern. Each project is different and should feel different.
5. **Pretty visuals, weak narrative.** Screenshots with no story connecting them.
6. **Overclaimed credit.** Taking full credit for team work.
7. **Fabricated metrics.** Suspiciously round numbers ("20% improvement"), metrics without baselines.
8. **No accessibility considerations.** This is table stakes now, not a bonus.
9. **No personality.** The case study sounds like it could have been written by anyone (or by an AI). The designer's voice, opinions, and taste should come through.

## What makes a portfolio memorable

Four things that consistently stand out:

### 1. Problem reframing
When the designer didn't just solve the brief but questioned it. Shows judgment, not just execution. Push the user: "Was the problem you solved the same problem you were given?"

### 2. Visible struggle
Showing the messy middle, rejected directions, and the logic for killing them. This is more convincing than any polished final. Push: "What didn't work? What was the hard part?"

### 3. Honest outcomes
Specific numbers with baselines beat vague gains. And honest "this didn't ship because leadership pivoted" beats fabricated metrics. Push: "Do you have real evidence of impact? If not, let's be honest about it."

### 4. Genuine reflection
Not a section to check off, but evidence that the designer learns from their work and carries it forward. "What would you do differently" is the simplest version. "How did this change how you think about design" is the deeper one.

## Portfolio-level guidance

If the user asks about their portfolio overall:

- **3 to 5 case studies, not 10.** Depth beats breadth.
- **Lead with the strongest.** Reviewers often only read one fully.
- **Vary the project types.** Don't show five redesigns of the same thing.
- **Each case study should feel editorially distinct.** Different structure, different emphasis, different tone. The portfolio itself should demonstrate range.
- **First impressions are fast.** The hero shot and first paragraph decide if someone keeps reading.

## Company culture context

Different companies value different things. Worth knowing if the user is targeting a specific kind of role:

- **Google**: end-to-end thinking, data-informed decisions, collaboration evidence
- **Apple**: craft and restraint, editorial judgment about what to remove
- **Stripe**: minimal, strategy-forward, business framing first
- **Meta**: clearly articulated problems, explorations, final walkthrough
- **Linear/Vercel/Figma**: shipped product as portfolio, craft-essay format, link to live work
- **Startups**: speed, scrappiness, wearing multiple hats, shipping with constraints
